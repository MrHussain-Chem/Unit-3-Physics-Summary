Content Area A: Waves & Optical Fibres

Periodic time: Time taken for one complete oscillation or wave cycle
Wave speed: Distance travelled by a wave per unit time
Wavelength (Œª): Distance between two consecutive points in phase on adjacent waves
Frequency (f): Number of complete oscillations or wave cycles per second (Hz)
Amplitude: Maximum displacement from equilibrium position
The Wave Equation:
v = fŒª
where v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), Œª = wavelength (m)
Example: Calculate wave speed
A wave has frequency 50 Hz and wavelength 6 m

Always include units in your final answer. Remember: v in m/s, f in Hz, Œª in m.
Transverse waves:
  • Oscillations perpendicular to direction of energy transfer
  • Examples: electromagnetic waves, water waves, waves on strings
  • Can be polarized
Longitudinal waves:
  • Oscillations parallel to direction of energy transfer
  • Examples: sound waves, ultrasound, seismic P-waves
  • Consist of compressions and rarefactions
  • Cannot be polarized
üí° Application Tip:
Remember which is which: TransVERSE has oscillations perpendicULAR to wave direction.
Coherence: Waves have constant phase difference
Path difference: Difference in distance travelled by two waves
Phase difference: How much one wave is ahead or behind another (measured in degrees or radians)
Superposition: When two or more waves meet, the resultant displacement is the sum of individual displacements
Diffraction gratings:
  • Multiple slits that diffract light
  • Produce line emission spectra
  • Each element produces unique pattern of spectral lines
  • Used to identify elements in gases
Emission spectra:
  • Produced when electrons drop from higher to lower energy levels
  • Energy is released as photons of specific frequencies
  • Different transitions produce different wavelengths (colors)
  • Unique pattern for each element - like a fingerprint
üí° Application Tip:
Line spectra are used in astronomy to identify elements in stars and in chemical analysis.
Stationary wave: Wave formed by superposition of two progressive waves of same frequency travelling in opposite directions
Resonance: Maximum amplitude oscillation when driving frequency equals natural frequency of system
Features of stationary waves:
  • Nodes: Points of zero amplitude (destructive interference)
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude (constructive interference)
  • Energy is not transferred along the wave
  • All points between nodes oscillate in phase
Applications in musical instruments:
  • Strings: Guitar strings, violin strings vibrate in modes
  • Pipes: Wind instruments, organ pipes produce resonant frequencies
  • Fundamental frequency and harmonics determine pitch

üí° Application Tip:
In exams, be able to label nodes and antinodes on stationary wave diagrams and explain how they form.
Refraction: Change in direction of wave when it passes from one medium to another due to change in speed
Total internal reflection (TIR): Complete reflection of light at boundary between two media when angle of incidence exceeds critical angle
Critical angle: Angle of incidence in denser medium for which angle of refraction is 90¬∞
Refractive index equations:
n = c/v
where n = refractive index, c = speed of light in vacuum, v = speed of light in medium
n = sin i / sin r
where i = angle of incidence, r = angle of refraction
sin c = 1/n
where c = critical angle, n = refractive index
Conditions for TIR:
  1. Light must travel from more optically dense to less optically dense medium
  2. Angle of incidence must be greater than critical angle

Optical fibre structure:
  • Core: Inner part where light travels (higher refractive index)
  • Cladding: Outer layer (lower refractive index)
  • Cladding allows TIR at smaller angles, keeping light in core
Applications:
  • Communications: Internet, telephone cables (high speed data)
  • Medicine: Endoscopes for internal examinations
  • Engineering: Inspection of difficult-to-reach areas
üí° Application Tip:
Remember: TIR only works going from dense to less dense. Critical angle depends on refractive index.
Key facts:
  • All EM waves travel at speed of light in vacuum: c = 3.0 √ó 10‚Å∏ m/s
  • EM spectrum regions overlap
  • Different frequencies and wavelengths used for different purposes
Inverse square law:
I = k/r¬≤
where I = intensity, k = constant, r = distance from source

Content Area B: Forces & Motion

Standard SI units (must know):
  • u = initial velocity (m/s)
  • v = final velocity (m/s)
  • s = distance/displacement (m)
  • t = time (s)
  • a = acceleration (m/s¬≤)
Speed units:
  • m/s (metres per second)
  • km/s (kilometres per second)
  • km/h (kilometres per hour)
Key formulae:
speed = distance √∑ time
average speed = total distance √∑ total time
Example: Calculate average speed
A car travels 150 km in 2 hours
Average speed = 150 √∑ 2 = 75 km/h
Scalar quantity: Has magnitude only (e.g., distance, speed, mass, time, energy)
Vector quantity: Has both magnitude AND direction (e.g., displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum)
Displacement vs Distance:
  • Distance: Total path length travelled (scalar)
  • Displacement: Straight line distance from start to finish with direction (vector)
Velocity vs Speed:
  • Speed: Rate of change of distance (scalar)
  • Velocity: Rate of change of displacement, includes direction (vector)
üí° Application Tip:
In exams, always state whether a quantity is scalar or vector if asked. Direction matters for vectors!
Displacement-time graphs:
  • Gradient = velocity
  • Horizontal line = stationary (zero velocity)
  • Straight sloped line = constant velocity
  • Curved line = changing velocity (acceleration)
Velocity-time graphs:
  • Gradient = acceleration
  • Area under graph = displacement
  • Horizontal line = constant velocity
  • Straight sloped line = constant acceleration
  • Curved line = changing acceleration
Acceleration formula from graph:
a = (v - u) / t
where a = acceleration, v = final velocity, u = initial velocity, t = time
üí° Application Tip:
Practice reading graphs! Know what gradient and area represent for each type of graph.
The SUVAT equations (must memorize):

Example: Calculate final velocity
A car accelerates from rest (u = 0) at 2 m/s¬≤ for 10 seconds

üí° Application Tip:
Choose the right equation! List what you know (u, v, a, s, t) and pick equation with 4 of those 5 variables.
Newton's First Law:
An object will remain at rest or continue moving at constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant force.
Inertia: Resistance of an object to change in motion - greater mass = greater inertia
Applications:
  • Objects at rest stay at rest (need force to start moving)
  • Objects moving continue moving at same velocity (unless force acts)
  • Explains why seatbelts needed - body continues forward when car stops
üí° Application Tip:
Resultant force = 0 means constant velocity OR stationary, NOT necessarily zero velocity!
Mass: Amount of matter in object, measured in kg (scalar)
Weight: Force due to gravity acting on mass, measured in N (vector)
Gravitational field strength (g): Force per unit mass, on Earth g ‚âà 10 N/kg or 9.8 N/kg
Weight equation:
W = mg
where W = weight (N), m = mass (kg), g = gravitational field strength (N/kg)
Example: Calculate weight
A person has mass 70 kg, g = 10 N/kg
W = mg = 70 √ó 10 = 700 N
Coefficient of friction (Œº):
F = ŒºN
where F = frictional force, Œº = coefficient of friction, N = normal reaction force
Types of friction:
  • Static friction: Force needed to START movement (Œº measured just before object moves)
  • Dynamic/kinetic friction: Force needed to KEEP moving at constant velocity
  • Static friction coefficient is usually greater than kinetic
üí° Application Tip:
On horizontal surface, normal reaction N = weight W. For friction, use F = ŒºN.
Momentum: Product of mass and velocity, p = mv, measured in kg m/s (vector quantity)
Newton's Second Law:
Force is proportional to rate of change of momentum
F = (mv - mu) / t
Force = change in momentum / time
F = ma
For constant mass, force = mass √ó acceleration
Example: Calculate force
A car of mass 1000 kg accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s in 5 seconds
F = (mv - mu) / t = (1000 √ó 20 - 0) / 5 = 20000 / 5 = 4000 N
Or: a = (v-u)/t = 20/5 = 4 m/s¬≤, F = ma = 1000 √ó 4 = 4000 N
Impact force controls in vehicles:
  • Air bags: Increase time of collision, reducing force
  • Seat belts: Increase time to stop, spread force over body
  • Crumple zones: Increase collision time, absorb energy
  • Helmets: Increase impact time, reduce force on head
Key principle: Same change in momentum over LONGER time = SMALLER force
üí° Application Tip:
High speed + low mass OR low speed + high mass both give large momentum. Safety devices increase impact time.
Newton's Third Law:
For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction
Key points:
  • Action and reaction forces are equal in magnitude
  • Forces act in opposite directions
  • Forces act on DIFFERENT objects
  • Forces are same type (both contact or both non-contact)
Resultant force and motion:
  • F = 0 (balanced forces): Constant velocity OR stationary
  • F ‚â† 0 (unbalanced forces): Object accelerates in direction of resultant force
Terminal velocity:
  • Occurs when drag force = weight
  • Resultant force = 0, so no acceleration
  • Object falls at constant maximum velocity
  • Applications: falling parachutes, vehicles at maximum speed, objects in liquids
üí° Application Tip:
Terminal velocity: weight down = drag up, forces balanced, constant velocity NOT zero velocity.

Content Area C: Electrical Circuits & Energy

Must know circuit symbols for:
  • Cell, battery, power supply
  • Switch (open/closed)
  • Lamp/bulb
  • Fixed resistor, variable resistor
  • Ammeter, voltmeter
  • Diode, LED
  • Thermistor, LDR (Light Dependent Resistor)
Key terminology:
  • Current (I): Rate of flow of charge, measured in amperes/amps (A)
  • Potential difference/Voltage (V): Energy transferred per unit charge, measured in volts (V)
  • Resistance (R): Opposition to current flow, measured in ohms (Œ©)
  • Power (P): Rate of energy transfer, measured in watts (W)
  • Energy (E): Measured in joules (J)
Measuring in circuits:
  • Ammeter: Connected in SERIES to measure current
  • Voltmeter: Connected in PARALLEL to measure potential difference
  • Ohmmeter: Measures resistance of component
üí° Application Tip:
Remember: Ammeter in Series, Voltmeter in Parallel. Current is SAME in series, Voltage is SAME in parallel.
Filament lamp:
  • Resistance increases with temperature
  • Current increases, lamp heats up, resistance increases
  • Non-linear I-V characteristic
Diode:
  • Allows current in one direction only
  • High resistance in reverse direction
  • Used for rectification (converting AC to DC)
Thermistor:
  • Resistance decreases as temperature increases
  • Used in temperature sensors, thermostats
Light Dependent Resistor (LDR):
  • Resistance decreases as light intensity increases
  • Used in automatic lighting, light sensors
Light Emitting Diode (LED):
  • Emits light when current flows through
  • Low power consumption
  • Used in displays, indicators, lighting
üí° Application Tip:
Know how resistance changes: Thermistor and LDR resistance DECREASES with temp/light increase.
Essential formulae (must memorize):
V = IR
Voltage = Current √ó Resistance (Ohm's Law)
P = IV
Power = Current √ó Voltage
P = E/t
Power = Energy / time
E = VIt
Energy = Voltage √ó Current √ó time
Example: Calculate current
A 12V battery powers a 4Œ© resistor
V = IR, so I = V/R = 12/4 = 3 A
Example: Calculate power
A lamp operates at 230V with current 0.5A
P = IV = 0.5 √ó 230 = 115 W
Example: Calculate energy transferred
A 12V, 2A device runs for 5 minutes (300s)
E = VIt = 12 √ó 2 √ó 300 = 7200 J
üí° Application Tip:
Triangle method: Cover what you want to find, what's left is the formula. For V=IR: cover V, you see I√óR.
Calculating energy in kilowatt-hours (kWh):
Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) √ó time (h)
Example: Calculate energy usage
A 2kW heater runs for 3 hours
Energy = 2 √ó 3 = 6 kWh
If electricity costs 15p per kWh: Cost = 6 √ó 15 = 90p
Converting between units:
  • 1 kWh = 1000 W √ó 3600 s = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 MJ
  • To convert kW to W: multiply by 1000
  • To convert hours to seconds: multiply by 3600
Fuses:
  • Safety device that melts if current too high
  • Choose fuse rating just above normal operating current
  • Common ratings: 3A, 5A, 13A
  • Calculate: I = P/V to find current, then choose next fuse up
Example: Choose correct fuse
A 2300W kettle operates at 230V
I = P/V = 2300/230 = 10A
Choose 13A fuse (next size up from 10A)
üí° Application Tip:
For domestic appliances, calculate current then choose 3A, 5A or 13A fuse - whichever is next up.

Specific latent heat: Energy needed to change state of 1 kg of substance at constant temperature, units: J kg‚Åª¬π
Specific latent heat of fusion: Energy to change 1 kg solid to liquid at melting point
Specific latent heat of vaporisation: Energy to change 1 kg liquid to gas at boiling point
Latent heat equation:
ŒîQ = mL
where ŒîQ = thermal energy (J), m = mass (kg), L = specific latent heat (J kg‚Åª¬π)

Key points about state changes:
  • Temperature stays constant during state change
  • Energy goes into breaking/forming bonds, not increasing temperature
  • Latent heat of vaporisation > latent heat of fusion
  • More energy needed to boil than to melt
üí° Application Tip:
During melting/boiling, temperature constant even though energy being added. Energy breaks bonds.

‚ùå Error #1: Missing or incorrect units

Especially common in physics! Every answer needs correct units.

‚úì Fix: Write units next to every number in working AND in final answer

‚ùå Error #2: Not showing working

Physics calculations MUST show every step to get method marks.

‚úì Fix: Write formula, substitute values with units, show calculation

‚ùå Error #3: Using wrong formula

Students often mix up similar formulae or use wrong SUVAT equation.

‚úì Fix: List known variables (u,v,a,s,t), identify what's missing, choose equation with 4 of the 5

‚ùå Error #4: Confusing scalar and vector

Mixing up distance/displacement or speed/velocity.

‚úì Fix: Remember vectors need direction. Displacement and velocity are vectors.

‚ùå Error #5: Not converting units

Using km/h with m/s, or minutes with seconds, or cm with m.

‚úì Fix: Convert ALL to standard SI units BEFORE calculating

‚ùå Error #6: Sign errors in calculations

Especially with acceleration and forces in opposite directions.

‚úì Fix: Define positive direction first, use negative for opposite direction

‚ùå Error #7: Misreading graphs

Confusing what gradient and area represent on different graphs.

‚úì Fix: Displacement-time: gradient = velocity. Velocity-time: gradient = acceleration, area = distance

‚ùå Error #8: Incorrect significant figures

Giving too many or too few significant figures in final answer.

‚úì Fix: Match significant figures to data given (usually 2 or 3 SF)
